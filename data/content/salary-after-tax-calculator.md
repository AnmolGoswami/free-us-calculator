### 2026 FICA Taxes — Unchanged Rates, Higher Wage Base

| Tax | Rate | Wage Limit |
|---|---|---|
| Social Security (Employee) | 6.2% | First $184,500 of gross wages |
| Medicare (Employee) | 1.45% | No limit |

## How the 2026 Take-Home Pay Calculation Works — Step by Step

This is the exact sequence this calculator uses, following the IRS methodology:

**Step 1 — Start with Gross Annual Salary**
Your total compensation before any deductions.

**Step 2 — Subtract Pre-Tax Deductions to Get AGI**
Pre-tax contributions reduce your Adjusted Gross Income (AGI) and your federal and state taxable income. They do **not** reduce your Social Security and Medicare tax base — FICA is calculated on gross wages regardless of 401(k) or HSA contributions.

Pre-tax deductions include:
- 401(k) / 403(b) / 457 contributions (up to $24,500 in 2026; $32,500 if age 50+; $35,750 if age 60–63)
- Traditional IRA contributions (up to $7,500 in 2026; $8,600 if age 50+)
- HSA contributions ($4,400 individual / $8,750 family in 2026)
- FSA contributions (up to $3,400 in 2026)
- Employer-sponsored health, dental, and vision insurance premiums paid pre-tax

**Step 3 — Subtract Standard Deduction to Get Taxable Income**
For most salaried employees, the standard deduction is the right choice. Only about 10.8% of US taxpayers itemize. For reference: itemizing makes sense when your total qualifying deductions (mortgage interest, state/local taxes up to $10,000, charitable contributions, etc.) exceed your standard deduction amount.

**Step 4 — Apply Federal Tax Brackets Progressively**
Tax is calculated on each layer of taxable income at the corresponding rate. The calculator does this in full, not just your marginal rate.

**Step 5 — Subtract Tax Credits**
Tax credits reduce your actual tax bill dollar-for-dollar. Major credits in 2026:

- **Child Tax Credit:** $2,000 per qualifying child under 17; refundable portion up to $1,700 per child
- **Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC):** Up to $664 (no children), $4,427 (1 child), $7,329 (2 children), $8,046 (3+ children)
- **Child and Dependent Care Credit:** Up to $3,000 (1 qualifying person), $6,000 (2+ persons), credit rate 20–35%
- **American Opportunity Tax Credit:** Up to $2,500 per eligible student (first 4 years of higher education); 40% refundable
- **Lifetime Learning Credit:** Up to $2,000 per return for qualified education expenses
- **Saver's Credit:** 10–50% of retirement contributions, up to $2,000 (single) / $4,000 (joint), for lower-income filers

**Step 6 — Calculate FICA Taxes**
Applied to gross wages — not reduced by pre-tax deductions. Social Security: 6.2% up to $184,500. Medicare: 1.45% on everything. Additional Medicare: 0.9% on income above thresholds.

**Step 7 — Add State Income Tax**
Applied to state taxable income, which follows state-specific rules that often differ from federal AGI. Nine states have no income tax; others range from flat rates to multi-bracket progressive systems reaching over 13% (California).

**Step 8 — Final Net Take-Home Pay**
Gross salary minus federal income tax, FICA taxes, state income tax, and all pre-tax deductions equals your annual take-home pay. Divide by 12 for monthly, 26 for bi-weekly, 52 for weekly.

### The Real Dollar Value of Maxing Your 401(k) in 2026

For a single filer earning $100,000 in a 22% federal bracket with 5% state tax:

- Max 401(k) contribution: $24,500
- Federal tax savings: $24,500 × 22% = **$5,390**
- State tax savings (5%): $24,500 × 5% = **$1,225**
- Total tax savings: **$6,615 per year**
- Monthly take-home reduction from the contribution: ($24,500 − $6,615) ÷ 12 = **~$1,491/month less in take-home**
- But you now have $24,500 growing in a tax-advantaged account

Translation: you invest $24,500 and it only costs your take-home $17,885 in after-tax dollars. The IRS and your state government effectively subsidize $6,615 of your retirement savings.

### OBBBA Tips and Overtime Deduction — New for 2025–2028

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act introduced two new above-the-line deductions for 2025–2028:

**Qualified Tips Deduction:** Eligible workers in tip-based industries (food service, hospitality, personal care) can deduct up to $25,000 in qualifying tip income from federal taxable income. The deduction phases out for higher-income earners. Tips must be voluntary customer payments — mandatory service charges do not qualify.

**Qualified Overtime Deduction:** Workers who receive overtime pay can deduct up to $12,500 in qualified overtime compensation from federal taxable income (up to $25,000 for joint filers). This applies to overtime paid under the FLSA. The deduction phases out starting at $150,000 AGI (single) / $300,000 (joint).

Both deductions are above-the-line — meaning they reduce your AGI regardless of whether you take the standard deduction or itemize. They are not factored into most salary calculators yet.

## Marginal vs Effective Tax Rate — The Most Misunderstood Concept in US Personal Finance

Your **marginal tax rate** is the rate applied to the last dollar you earned. If you are a single filer with $100,000 in taxable income, your marginal rate is 22% — the bracket covering income above $50,400.

Your **effective tax rate** is what you actually pay as a percentage of your total taxable income. It is always lower than your marginal rate because the lower brackets still apply to your first dollars of income.

### Effective Rate vs Marginal Rate — Single Filer 2026

| Gross Salary | Standard Deduction | Taxable Income | Federal Tax | Marginal Rate | Effective Rate |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| $30,000 | $16,100 | $13,900 | $1,390 + $180 = $1,570 | 12% | 5.2% |
| $50,000 | $16,100 | $33,900 | $1,240 + $2,580 = $3,820 | 12% | 7.6% |
| $75,000 | $16,100 | $58,900 | $1,240 + $4,560 + $1,870 = $7,670 | 22% | 10.2% |
| $100,000 | $16,100 | $83,900 | $1,240 + $4,560 + $7,370 = $13,170 | 22% | 13.2% |
| $150,000 | $16,100 | $133,900 | Full brackets = ~$24,342 | 24% | 16.2% |
| $200,000 | $16,100 | $183,900 | Full brackets = ~$38,254 | 32% | 19.1% |
| $300,000 | $16,100 | $283,900 | Full brackets = ~$71,018 | 35% | 23.7% |

*Approximate figures. Use the calculator above for exact numbers including FICA and state tax.*

This table shows why the common fear that "a raise will push me into a higher bracket and I'll take home less" is mathematically impossible. Only the dollars above the bracket threshold are taxed at the higher rate. A raise always increases your net take-home pay.

## How to Maximize Your 2026 Take-Home Pay — Specific Strategies

### Strategy 1: Max Your 401(k) First, Always

If your employer matches contributions — typically 3–6% of salary — and you are not contributing enough to get the full match, you are leaving free money on the table. A 4% match on a $90,000 salary is $3,600 per year of employer contribution that disappears if not claimed. Every dollar of match is a 100% immediate return before any investment growth.

After capturing the full match, continue contributing to the 2026 limit of $24,500 if your cash flow allows.

### Strategy 2: Pair HSA with a High-Deductible Health Plan

The HSA is the only triple-tax-advantaged account in the US tax code:

- Contributions are pre-tax (reduce federal and state taxable income)
- Growth is tax-free
- Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are tax-free

At 22% federal + 5% state bracket, contributing the full $4,400 individual limit saves $1,188 in taxes immediately. Unused HSA funds roll over indefinitely and can be invested in mutual funds. After age 65, HSA funds can be withdrawn for any purpose (taxed as ordinary income, like a traditional IRA — but no penalty).

### Strategy 3: Verify Your W-4 Withholding Matches Your Real Tax Situation

The default W-4 withholding often results in either over-withholding (the IRS holds your money interest-free all year, returned as a refund) or under-withholding (you face a balance due plus possible underpayment penalty in April). Review your withholding once a year — especially after:

- A salary change (raise, promotion, new job)
- A major life change (marriage, divorce, child birth, home purchase)
- A change in filing status
- Starting a side business or freelance income

### Strategy 4: Check Whether Itemizing Beats the Standard Deduction

Only about 10.8% of taxpayers itemize — but for people with large mortgage interest, high state/local taxes (up to the $10,000 SALT cap), or significant charitable contributions, itemizing still beats the 2026 standard deduction. Run the comparison if your total qualifying deductions are close to your standard deduction amount.

### Strategy 5: Contribute to a Dependent Care FSA for Childcare Costs

If you have children in daycare or after-school care, the Dependent Care FSA allows up to $5,000 per household pre-tax. At a 24% federal bracket plus state tax, this saves approximately $1,450–$1,800 in combined taxes annually. This is separate from the child care tax credit, which covers a different portion of expenses.

### Strategy 6: Move to a No-Income-Tax State — The Numbers at High Salaries

The tax savings from relocating from a high-tax state to a no-tax state are substantial and often underestimated:

| Salary | California → Texas Annual Savings | New York → Florida Annual Savings |
|---|---|---|
| $100,000 | ~$5,000 – $7,000 | ~$4,500 – $6,000 |
| $150,000 | ~$11,000 – $15,000 | ~$9,000 – $12,000 |
| $200,000 | ~$16,000 – $21,000 | ~$13,000 – $17,000 |
| $300,000 | ~$25,000 – $32,000 | ~$19,000 – $25,000 |

These are annual savings in state income tax alone. The decision to relocate involves many other factors — but for remote workers, the tax arbitrage is real and compounding.

## W-4 and Paycheck Withholding — How to Stop Over- or Under-Paying

The W-4 form you fill out when starting a new job tells your employer how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck. Getting this right matters.

**Over-withholding:** You get a large refund in April. This sounds good but is not — you have loaned the government money interest-free all year. That money could have been in a high-yield savings account earning 4–5%.

**Under-withholding:** You owe money in April, potentially plus an underpayment penalty (currently ~7.5% of the underpaid amount per IRS). This happens most often when someone has two jobs, significant side income, investment income, or did not update their W-4 after a life change.

**The right approach:** Use the IRS Tax Withholding Estimator (irs.gov/W4app) at the start of each year. Enter your expected total income, filing status, deductions, and credits. Set your withholding to result in a small refund or small balance due — never a large refund, never a large balance due.

## Self-Employed and Freelancer Take-Home Pay — 2026

If you are self-employed, a 1099 contractor, or a freelancer, your take-home pay calculation differs significantly from a W-2 salaried employee:

**Self-Employment Tax:** You pay both the employee and employer portions of FICA — 15.3% total (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare) on 92.35% of net self-employment income. You can deduct half of this SE tax from your gross income as an above-the-line deduction.

**Quarterly Estimated Taxes:** If you expect to owe $1,000 or more in federal taxes, the IRS requires quarterly estimated payments. For 2026: Q1 due April 15, Q2 due June 16, Q3 due September 15, Q4 due January 15, 2027.

**Self-Employed Deductions:** You can deduct the employer-equivalent half of SE tax, health insurance premiums, home office expenses, business mileage (IRS rate: $0.725/mile in 2026), and retirement contributions via SEP-IRA (up to 25% of net self-employment income, maximum $69,000) or Solo 401(k) (up to $24,500 employee + 25% employer = up to $69,000 total).

**Set aside 28–32% of every payment** for taxes if you are self-employed. Failing to do this is the single most common financial mistake among new freelancers and gig workers.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I calculate my take-home pay after taxes in 2026?

Start with your gross annual salary. Subtract pre-tax deductions (401k, HSA, health insurance premiums) to get your adjusted gross income. Subtract the 2026 standard deduction ($16,100 single, $32,200 married joint, $24,150 head of household) to get taxable income. Apply the federal tax brackets progressively — 10% on the first $12,400, 12% up to $50,400, 22% up to $105,700, and so on. Subtract any tax credits. Add FICA taxes (6.2% Social Security up to $184,500 + 1.45% Medicare on all wages). Add your state income tax. The remainder is your net annual take-home pay. The calculator above does all of this automatically.

### What are the 2026 federal income tax brackets?

The 2026 federal tax brackets for single filers are: 10% on income up to $12,400, 12% from $12,401–$50,400, 22% from $50,401–$105,700, 24% from $105,701–$201,775, 32% from $201,776–$256,225, 35% from $256,226–$640,600, and 37% above $640,600. These were confirmed by IRS Revenue Procedure 2025-32 and made permanent by the One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) signed July 2025. Married filing jointly thresholds are approximately double the single amounts.

### What is the Social Security wage base for 2026?

The Social Security wage base for 2026 is $184,500, up from $176,100 in 2025. Employees pay 6.2% on wages up to this limit. Income above $184,500 is not subject to Social Security tax — but is still subject to Medicare tax (1.45% with no cap) and the Additional Medicare Tax (0.9% above $200,000 for single filers).

### How much is taken out of a $100,000 salary in taxes?

For a single filer in Texas with no pre-tax deductions and no tax credits in 2026: federal income tax approximately $13,608, FICA approximately $7,650, state tax $0. Total deductions approximately $21,258. Net take-home approximately $78,742 annually or $6,562 per month. In California, add approximately $6,858 in state tax — reducing monthly take-home to approximately $5,990.

### What is the 401(k) contribution limit for 2026?

The 401(k) employee contribution limit for 2026 is $24,500, up from $23,500 in 2025, per IRS Notice 2025-67. Workers aged 50–59 can contribute an additional $8,000 (total $32,500). Workers aged 60–63 can contribute an additional $11,250 under the SECURE 2.0 super catch-up (total $35,750). Workers aged 64 return to the $8,000 catch-up. These contributions are pre-tax and directly reduce your federal and state taxable income.

### What is the difference between marginal and effective tax rate?

Your marginal tax rate is the rate applied to the last dollar you earn — the top bracket you reach. Your effective tax rate is your total federal income tax divided by your total gross income — always lower than your marginal rate because lower brackets apply to your first dollars. A single filer earning $100,000 has a 22% marginal rate but pays only about 13–14% as an effective rate. You can never take home less money from a raise — a higher bracket only applies to dollars above the threshold.

### What is the HSA contribution limit for 2026?

The HSA contribution limit for 2026 is $4,400 for individual (self-only) coverage and $8,750 for family coverage, per IRS Revenue Procedure 2025-32. An additional $1,000 catch-up contribution is allowed for those aged 55 and older. HSA contributions are triple tax-advantaged: pre-tax going in, tax-free growth, and tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses.

### Which states have no income tax in 2026?

Nine states have no individual income tax on wages in 2026: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, New Hampshire (eliminated income tax on all income as of 2025), South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Washington, and Wyoming. Washington has a 7% capital gains tax on gains above $250,000 but no tax on wage income. New Hampshire previously taxed interest and dividend income but fully eliminated that tax.

### How much does the standard deduction save in taxes in 2026?

The 2026 standard deduction is $16,100 for single filers, $32,200 for married filing jointly, and $24,150 for heads of household. The tax savings depend on your marginal bracket. A single filer in the 22% bracket saves $16,100 × 22% = $3,542 in federal income tax from the standard deduction alone. In a 24% bracket, the savings are $3,864. In a 12% bracket, $1,932. These are significant — which is why itemizing only makes sense when your total qualifying deductions exceed these thresholds.

### Does a 401(k) contribution reduce FICA taxes?

No. 401(k) contributions, HSA contributions, and most other pre-tax deductions reduce your federal and state income tax base — but FICA taxes (Social Security and Medicare) are calculated on your gross wages before any retirement or HSA contributions. The only pre-tax deductions that reduce FICA are Section 125 cafeteria plan contributions (employer-sponsored health insurance premiums paid through a qualifying cafeteria plan, dependent care FSA).

### What is the new overtime deduction for 2026?

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) introduced a new qualified overtime deduction for 2025–2028. Workers can deduct up to $12,500 in qualified overtime pay from federal taxable income as an above-the-line deduction (up to $25,000 for married filing jointly). The deduction phases out starting at $150,000 AGI (single) / $300,000 (joint). Only overtime paid under FLSA requirements qualifies — not bonuses or supplemental pay classified as overtime.
