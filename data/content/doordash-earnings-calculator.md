## How DoorDash Pay Is Actually Structured

Every completed order pays you from three sources. Understanding each one matters because they behave completely differently depending on when and where you are dashing.

**Base pay** is the guaranteed floor DoorDash sets for each order. It runs from $2 to $10 or more, depending on estimated distance, the time the delivery will take, and something the platform calls "desirability" — essentially how many other drivers have already passed on the offer. An order that sits unclaimed sees its base pay increase automatically over time. That is the reason you occasionally see a two-mile order paying $8 in base: nobody wanted it, and the price kept rising until someone did.

**Tips** are where most real income comes from. The Gridwise median tip is $3.66 per delivery — about 49% of total order pay on an average run. Tips are also the most unpredictable part of your earnings. The same restaurant, same neighborhood, same time of day can produce a zero tip one run and an $8 tip the next. This is why experienced drivers treat an order showing no tip as a strong signal to decline. The customer has already indicated what they plan to pay, and completing the delivery will not change that.

**Peak Pay and promotions** stack on top of base when demand in a zone exceeds driver supply. You see these as dollar bonuses per delivery — typically $1 to $4 extra during genuine rush windows. Weekly challenges add bonus payouts for completing a set number of deliveries in a set time, though the danger is letting challenge pressure push you into accepting bad orders just to reach a threshold number.

One fact that surprises many first-year Dashers: DoorDash does not take a commission cut from your earnings the way Uber deducts from ride fares. You receive 100% of base pay and 100% of tips. Every dollar your take-home falls short of your gross comes from your own operating costs and tax obligations, not a platform fee.

## Where the Money Goes Before It Reaches You

A driver working 30 hours per week in a typical US suburban market might gross around $568 at the national average rate. That sounds workable until you account for what it cost to earn it.

**Fuel** at 30 hours per week means covering roughly 400 to 450 miles. At current US average gas prices near $3.35 per gallon with a midsize sedan averaging 28 MPG, that is $43 to $58 per week straight out of your pocket. A hybrid cuts this nearly in half. A full EV at $0.16 per kWh for the same mileage costs roughly $18 to $22 per week — about 65% less than running a gas car.

**Maintenance** accelerates dramatically under delivery driving conditions. Frequent cold starts, constant stop-and-go city patterns, and high annual mileage combine to produce real repair costs. For a Dasher covering 20,000 business miles per year, expect $730 to $1,240 in oil changes, tires, brake service, and miscellaneous parts. Tires wear 35 to 45% faster than they would under normal driving. Brakes need replacement every 18 to 24 months instead of the typical four or five years.

**Depreciation** is the invisible drain that never appears as a line item. High delivery mileage destroys resale value. A vehicle dropping from 30,000 to 55,000 miles in one year of heavy dashing typically loses $2,500 to $4,500 in what it would sell for. Spread across 52 weeks, that is $48 to $87 per week in real wealth draining quietly away — until you try to sell and the number hits you. The 2026 IRS mileage rate of $0.725 per mile exists specifically to account for this cost.

**Insurance** is non-negotiable. Most standard personal auto policies exclude commercial driving. If you are involved in an accident while making a delivery without commercial coverage disclosed to your insurer, the claim can be denied. A rideshare and delivery rider added to your existing policy costs $10 to $30 per month. That is your minimum required investment for proper protection.

All of these combined add up to $125 to $207 per week in real costs before a single dollar of income tax. On $568 gross, you are left with roughly $280 to $360 in actual take-home. The calculator above will show you your specific number based on your real inputs.

## The 2026 IRS Mileage Deduction: The Biggest Money Most Dashers Leave Behind

The IRS standard mileage rate for 2026 is **$0.725 per mile**, confirmed in IRS Rev. Proc. 2025-18. This single deduction covers fuel, oil changes, tire wear, insurance, and vehicle depreciation — all bundled into one number you claim on Schedule C at tax time.

The dollar value of that deduction depends on how many miles you track:

- 10,000 annual business miles: **$7,250 deduction**
- 15,000 miles: **$10,875 deduction**
- 20,000 miles: **$14,500 deduction**

At the combined effective tax rate most Dashers pay — around 25 to 28% — tracking 15,000 miles correctly saves approximately $2,700 to $3,045 in real taxes. Drivers who skip mileage tracking pay that full amount to the IRS unnecessarily every single year.

### Which Miles Actually Count as Deductible?

IRS Publication 463 defines this precisely. Most Dashers claim only restaurant-to-customer miles and miss 25 to 40% of what they can legally deduct.

**Deductible:** Miles from your location to the first pickup once you have accepted an order. Miles from restaurant to customer. Miles between orders while actively logged in. Miles for business errands like a mechanic visit or car wash used for your delivery work.

**Not deductible:** The drive from home to your starting area before accepting any order. Miles after logging off. Personal trips mixed into a working shift.

The gap between "delivery miles only" and "all properly deductible miles" typically adds 25 to 40% more mileage to your return. On 12,000 claimed delivery miles, correct tracking often reveals 16,000 or more fully deductible miles — worth an additional $2,900 in deductions, or around $800 in actual tax savings you would otherwise lose.

The DoorDash app's own mileage summary only tracks active delivery legs and consistently underestimates total deductible miles by 25 to 40%. Use Stride (free, GPS auto-detection, IRS-compliant exports) or Everlance ($8 per month with expense tracking added). Start it when you leave home for your first dash. Every mile from your front door counts once you have an accepted order.

## Self-Employment Tax: The Part That Surprises Every First-Year Dasher

When you work a regular job, your employer pays half of your Social Security and Medicare taxes. As an independent contractor, you pay both halves yourself. That is the 15.3% self-employment tax — and it applies before federal income tax stacks on top of it.

Here is the exact step-by-step calculation so you have no surprises at filing time.

For a driver with $20,000 in net profit after expenses:

SE tax base is $20,000 multiplied by 92.35%, giving $18,470. SE tax owed is $18,470 multiplied by 15.3%, giving $2,826. You can deduct half of that SE tax from your gross income before calculating federal income tax, which reduces your adjusted gross by $1,413. After that deduction and the $15,000 standard deduction for single filers in 2026, your taxable federal income is $3,587. Federal income tax at the 10% bracket adds $359. **Total tax bill: approximately $3,185, or about 16% of net profit.**

That is manageable — but only if you have been setting it aside throughout the year rather than spending it. The Dashers who face a crisis in April are the ones who saw $20,000 enter their accounts and treated all of it as income available to spend.

**New for 2026:** The Qualified Tips Deduction, introduced for tax year 2025 and confirmed through 2028, allows eligible delivery workers to deduct up to **$25,000 in qualifying tip income** from federal taxable income as an above-the-line adjustment. A driver who earned $8,000 in tips in 2025 can deduct all $8,000, saving roughly $2,400 in taxes at a 30% combined rate. Most tax guides have not caught up to this yet — make sure yours has.

Move **25 to 28% of every DoorDash payout** into a separate savings account the same day it arrives. Do not wait for quarterly deadlines.

## How the Top 20% of Dashers Earn More Per Hour

The difference between netting $9 per hour and $17 per hour is not market or luck. It is three operating decisions applied without exception.

**The per-mile filter.** A $9 order requiring 7 miles is $1.29 per mile. After $5.08 in IRS-rate vehicle costs, you net $3.92 to cover your time on a probably 25-minute run. That is losing math. A $7 order requiring 2 miles is $3.50 per mile. Vehicle cost is $1.45, leaving $5.55 on an 8-minute delivery. That is winning math. Use **$1.50 per mile as your floor, $2.00 as your target**. Gridwise data confirms drivers maintaining 60 to 80% acceptance rates earn 15 to 25% more per hour than those accepting 95% or more of all offers. DoorDash does not deactivate drivers for low acceptance rates as of 2026.

**Peak hours without compromise.** Gridwise earnings data shows Sunday 6 to 8 PM averaging $18.28 per hour gross — the single best window nationally. Friday 6 to 8 PM comes in at $17.42. Weeknight dinner from 5:30 to 9:30 PM consistently delivers $15 to $17 per hour. A driver working 25 peak hours will out-earn a driver working 40 off-peak hours in total weekly income, while also logging fewer miles and putting less wear on their vehicle.

**Multi-apping.** Running DoorDash alongside Uber Eats simultaneously eliminates dead time between orders — the single biggest drag on effective hourly rate. When DoorDash sends a below-threshold offer, decline it and wait for Uber Eats. A 2024 Gridwise study found multi-apping drivers earned 31% more per hour than single-platform drivers. More than 65% of full-time gig workers now use two or more apps simultaneously.
