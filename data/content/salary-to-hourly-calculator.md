## The Formula (and Why 2,080 Matters)

The math is simple. The detail people miss is the denominator.

**Hourly Rate = Annual Salary ÷ (Hours per Week × Weeks per Year)**

The standard full-time year in the United States is **2,080 hours** — 40 hours per week multiplied by 52 weeks. Most people intuitively divide by 2,000 and get a number that's slightly too high. On a $100,000 salary the difference is $2.31 per hour. Small on paper, but worth knowing.

The more important number is your **actual** hours per week — not your contracted hours. A salaried role that says "40 hours" but consistently runs 50 is paying you $9.62 less per hour on a $100,000 salary than the standard calculation shows. Over a year, that's a $20,000 difference in what your time is actually worth.

The calculator above lets you adjust weekly hours precisely for this reason. Use 40 for a clean benchmark. Use your real average for an honest number.

The 2,080-hour standard is a myth for a significant portion of the American workforce.

According to Gallup, full-time salaried workers in the US average approximately 47 hours per week — not 40. That means the salary-to-hourly conversion most people use is wrong by 17.5%. Here's what that does to your real rate:

## Adjusting for Paid Time Off — Your Effective Hourly Rate Is Higher Than You Think

Most people forget that paid time off (PTO) changes the equation in your favor when you're salaried.

If you receive two weeks of paid vacation and work the other 50 weeks, you're effectively getting paid for time you don't work. That raises your real hourly rate on actual working hours:

**Example: $80,000 salary with 2 weeks PTO**

- Working weeks: 50
- Actual working hours: 50 × 40 = 2,000 hours
- Effective hourly rate: $80,000 ÷ 2,000 = **$40.00/hr** (vs $38.46 on 2,080 hours)

With 15 days PTO and 11 federal holidays (the average for US workers), you're down to about 234 working days or 1,872 working hours:

- Effective hourly rate: $80,000 ÷ 1,872 = **$42.74/hr**

This is the honest number to use when comparing salaried work to freelance or hourly contract work — because freelancers don't get paid on their days off. That gap has to be covered somewhere in your rate.

## Salary vs Hourly: Which Is Actually Better in 2026?

The right answer depends on your job, your hours, and whether you're exempt from overtime rules under the FLSA.

### Salaried (Exempt) Positions

A salaried exempt employee receives a fixed annual pay regardless of hours worked. Under the **Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA)**, to qualify as exempt from overtime requirements, all three of the following must be true:

- Paid on a salary basis (not by the hour)
- Earns at least **$684 per week ($35,568/year)** at the federal level — the current threshold as of 2026 after the DOL's higher threshold was vacated by a federal court in November 2024 and appeal remains pending
- Performs duties that meet the executive, administrative, professional, computer, or outside sales exemption under 29 CFR §541

**State thresholds are often higher.** California requires at least twice the state minimum wage for a 40-hour week to qualify for state overtime exemption — currently **$1,352/week ($70,304/year)** as of January 2026. Computer software employees in California must earn at least **$58.85/hour** to qualify for the computer professional exemption.

**What salaried exempt means in practice:** Your employer does not pay overtime for hours above 40. If your company culture runs 50-hour weeks, your effective hourly rate drops by 20% with no additional compensation. This is legal. It's also the reason calculating your real hourly rate based on actual hours matters so much before accepting a salaried offer.

**What salaried roles typically include that hourly roles don't:**
- Employer-sponsored health, dental, and vision insurance
- 401(k) with employer match (average US employer match is 4.7% of salary)
- Paid vacation and sick leave (average 10–15 days for new employees)
- Job stability and predictable income
- Career advancement pathways tied to performance reviews

### Hourly (Non-Exempt) Positions

Non-exempt hourly workers are entitled to overtime pay at **1.5× their regular rate** for every hour worked beyond 40 in a workweek. This is federal law under FLSA, and several states go further:

- **California and Alaska:** Daily overtime required (over 8 hours/day, even if weekly total is under 40)
- **Nevada:** Daily overtime over 8 hours if employee earns below 1.5× the state minimum wage
- **Colorado:** Daily overtime over 12 hours or 12-hour shift

For a $25/hour worker putting in 8 overtime hours per week, that's $300 in additional weekly earnings — $15,600 per year — that a salaried employee doing the same work would not receive.

### The Honest Comparison at $65,000

Imagine two candidates for equivalent work:

**Candidate A — Salaried, $65,000/year:** Typically works 48 hours/week. Effective rate = $65,000 ÷ 2,496 = **$26.04/hr**. Gets health insurance (value ~$6,000/year), 401(k) match 4% ($2,600/year), 10 days PTO.

**Candidate B — Hourly, $31.25/hr (same gross at 40 hrs):** Works exactly 40 hours. Earns the same $65,000. No overtime, no health insurance, no 401(k) match, no paid leave.

To match Candidate A's total compensation, Candidate B needs to earn more like **$35–$38/hour** before overtime. This is the math freelancers and contractors are supposed to use when setting rates — and most don't.

## How to Set Your Freelance Rate Using This Calculator

If you're moving from salaried employment to freelance, the biggest mistake is quoting your hourly salary equivalent and calling it your rate.

Freelancers and independent contractors need to cover costs that employers absorb for salaried workers. Here's the full markup formula:

**Step 1 — Start with your target equivalent salary**
Say you want to match $90,000/year in total compensation.

**Step 2 — Calculate equivalent gross hourly rate**
$90,000 ÷ 2,080 = $43.27/hour

**Step 3 — Add self-employment tax load**
Self-employed workers pay both the employee and employer halves of FICA — 15.3% total vs 7.65% for W-2 employees. Add roughly 7.65% to your rate to cover the employer half:
$43.27 × 1.0765 = **$46.59/hour**

**Step 4 — Add benefits replacement costs**
- Health insurance (individual market, no employer subsidy): $400–$800/month → $4,800–$9,600/year → $2.31–$4.62/hr added
- No paid leave: if you want 2 weeks equivalent, add ~4% → $1.73/hr
- No retirement match: if you want a 4% equivalent, add → $1.73/hr

**Step 5 — Add business overhead and non-billable time**
In most freelance businesses, 20–30% of your time is non-billable (admin, sales, gaps between clients). Divide by 0.75 (assuming 75% billable utilization):
$52 ÷ 0.75 = **$69.33/hour**

**Step 6 — Add profit margin**
Freelance is running a business. A 15–20% profit margin is reasonable:
$69.33 × 1.20 = **$83.20/hour**

So the correct freelance rate to match a $90,000 salaried role with benefits is roughly **$80–$90/hour** — not $43.

This is why experienced consultants and contractors charge rates that look high compared to equivalent salaries. They're not overcharging — they're pricing correctly.

## The OBBBA Overtime Deduction — New for 2025–2028

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA), signed July 2025, introduced a new **no-tax-on-overtime** provision for non-exempt hourly workers. This directly affects the real after-tax value of hourly positions with significant overtime.

Under IRC § 225, qualified overtime pay can be deducted from federal taxable income:
- **Up to $12,500** for single filers and married filing separately
- **Up to $25,000** for married filing jointly
- Phase-out begins at $150,000 MAGI (single) / $300,000 (joint)
- Only applies to **FLSA-required overtime** for non-exempt employees — not voluntary overtime for exempt salaried workers, not contractor income

**What this means in real dollars:**
A non-exempt worker earning $28/hour in a 22% federal bracket who works 8 overtime hours per week:
- Annual overtime earnings: 8 hrs × 52 weeks × ($28 × 1.5) = $17,472
- Deductible amount: $12,500 (cap for single filer)
- Federal tax savings: $12,500 × 22% = **$2,750/year**

This effectively makes overtime more valuable per dollar than it was before 2026 for non-exempt workers below the phase-out thresholds. When comparing salaried exempt vs hourly non-exempt offers, factor this in.

## Real-World Examples — Salary to Hourly in Context

### Example 1 — The Tech Job That Sounds Better Than It Is

A software engineer in Seattle receives two offers:

- **Offer A:** $130,000 salary, exempt, standard 40-hour week on paper but team culture averages 50+ hours
- **Offer B:** $110,000 salary, same company, different team known for strict 40-hour culture

At 50 hours/week: Offer A = $130,000 ÷ 2,600 = **$50.00/hr**
At 40 hours/week: Offer B = $110,000 ÷ 2,080 = **$52.88/hr**

The $20,000 lower salary offer actually pays more per hour. Before accepting Offer A, ask current team members: "What time do people typically leave the office on a Tuesday?" Their answer tells you more than the job description.

### Example 2 — The Nurse Evaluating Shift Differentials

A registered nurse earning $95,000 in California regularly works 12-hour shifts totaling 48 hours per week.

Standard calculation: $95,000 ÷ 2,080 = $45.67/hr
Actual rate at 48 hrs: $95,000 ÷ 2,496 = **$38.06/hr**

Her hospital is offering a night shift differential of $6/hour for the same schedule. That brings effective compensation to $95,000 + (8 overtime equivalent × 52 weeks × $6) = $95,000 + $2,496 = $97,496 actual. Her real hourly: **$39.06/hr**.

For shift workers, always calculate total compensation including differentials before comparing to any straight-salary role.

### Example 3 — The Freelancer Setting Rates

A graphic designer leaving a $75,000 full-time job with full benefits in Austin, Texas.

Step 1: Target $75,000 equivalent
Step 2: Base rate = $75,000 ÷ 2,080 = $36.06/hr
Step 3: SE tax adjustment (+7.65%) = $38.82
Step 4: Health insurance (~$500/month) = +$2.88 → $41.70
Step 5: No paid leave (15 days) = +$1.51 → $43.21
Step 6: Non-billable time (25%) = $43.21 ÷ 0.75 = $57.61
Step 7: Profit margin (15%) = $57.61 × 1.15 = **$66.25/hour**

She should quote $65–$70/hour for client work, not $36. Anything less and she's taking a pay cut from her salaried job before factoring in the additional stress and income variability of freelancing.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### How do I convert annual salary to hourly rate?

Divide your annual salary by the total hours worked in a year. Standard full-time is 40 hours per week × 52 weeks = 2,080 hours. A $100,000 salary ÷ 2,080 = $48.08 per hour. If you regularly work more than 40 hours, use your actual average — the result tells you what your time is genuinely worth per hour, not just on paper.

### Is $100,000 a year a good hourly rate?

$100,000 a year is $48.08 per hour before taxes based on a standard 40-hour week. After federal income tax and FICA for a single filer in 2026, the net take-home is roughly $78,700/year in a no-income-tax state — around $37.84/hr net. By US standards, this is well above the national median and affords a comfortable lifestyle in most cities outside of New York and San Francisco.

### What is the hourly rate for $80,000 a year?

$80,000 per year equals $38.46 per hour at 40 hours per week. At 45 hours per week it falls to $34.19. After federal tax and FICA for a single filer in 2026 with no state income tax, the net hourly rate is approximately $31.11–$31.49 per hour. In California, subtract roughly $1.60–$1.70 per hour more for state income tax.

### How much should a freelancer charge compared to their salary?

A freelancer needs to charge significantly more than their salary-equivalent hourly rate to come out ahead financially. The general rule is to add 50–75% to your gross hourly rate to cover self-employment tax (15.3% vs 7.65% for W-2 employees), benefits you no longer receive (health insurance, retirement match, paid leave), and non-billable time (typically 20–30% of your hours). A $90,000 salary equivalent lands at roughly $80–$90/hour for a freelancer, not $43.

### Does salary or hourly pay more?

It depends entirely on hours worked, overtime eligibility, and benefits. Salaried exempt roles typically offer stability, benefits, and career growth but often involve unpaid overtime. Hourly non-exempt roles require overtime pay at 1.5× after 40 hours and offer more predictable earnings per hour worked. A salaried role that consistently runs 50 hours per week is often worth less per hour than it appears. Always calculate based on actual expected hours.

### What is the FLSA overtime threshold in 2026?

The federal FLSA salary threshold for overtime exemption is currently $684 per week ($35,568/year) as of 2026. The DOL's 2024 rule that raised this to $1,128/week ($58,656/year) was vacated by a federal court in November 2024, reverting the threshold. The appeal is pending. State thresholds are often higher — California requires at least twice the state minimum wage for a 40-hour week, which works out to approximately $70,304/year for state-level exemption in 2026.

### How does PTO affect my real hourly rate?

Paid time off increases your effective hourly rate on actual working hours. With two weeks PTO, you work 50 weeks instead of 52 — meaning your real hourly cost to your employer (and your effective earnings rate) is higher by about 4% than the standard calculation. A $80,000 salary with 2 weeks PTO = $80,000 ÷ 2,000 working hours = $40.00/hr, not $38.46. This is the number to use when comparing a salaried role with generous leave to a contract position with no paid time off.

### What is $25 an hour as an annual salary?

$25 per hour × 40 hours × 52 weeks = **$52,000 per year** gross. That's $4,333/month and $2,000 biweekly. After federal income tax and FICA (single filer, 2026, no state income tax), take-home is approximately $42,600–$43,400/year or about $3,550–$3,617 per month.

### What is $30 an hour as an annual salary?

$30 per hour × 2,080 hours = **$62,400 per year** gross. Monthly gross: $5,200. After taxes (single, no state income tax, 2026): approximately $50,700–$51,600/year or $4,225–$4,300 per month. $30/hour sits just above the US median hourly wage and is a solid benchmark for mid-career workers in most non-coastal markets.
