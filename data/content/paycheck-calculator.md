## How Your Paycheck is Calculated — The Complete Formula {#how-your-paycheck-is-calculated}

Every paycheck follows the same basic equation. It's not complicated once you see it laid out clearly:

```
Net Pay = Gross Pay − Federal Income Tax − State Income Tax 
          − FICA (Social Security + Medicare) − Pre-Tax Deductions 
          − Post-Tax Deductions − Local Taxes (where applicable)
```

Let's break each piece down.

**Gross Pay** is your total earnings before anything is removed — your annual salary divided by your number of pay periods, or your hourly rate multiplied by hours worked.

**Federal Income Tax** is calculated using progressive brackets based on your taxable income (gross pay minus your standard deduction and any pre-tax contributions).

**State Income Tax** depends entirely on where you live. Nine states collect zero. California goes up to 13.3%. The difference between living in Texas and California on a $100,000 salary can be $5,000–$8,000 per year in take-home pay.

**FICA** stands for Federal Insurance Contributions Act. It's split into Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%). Your employer matches both — but that match never shows up in your paycheck. You only see your half.

**Pre-Tax Deductions** — things like 401(k) contributions, HSA deposits, and health insurance premiums — come out before taxes are calculated, which means they reduce your taxable income and lower your tax bill at the same time.

**Post-Tax Deductions** (Roth 401(k), certain life insurance premiums) come out after taxes, so they don't reduce your taxable income.

**Local Taxes** apply in cities like New York City (up to 3.876%), Philadelphia (3.79%), Detroit (2.4%), and Louisville (2.28%). Many people don't realize these exist until their first paycheck arrives.

### Understanding Marginal vs Effective Tax Rate

This is one of the most misunderstood things about the US tax system. If you earn $60,000 filing single in 2026, you are NOT taxed at 22% on your whole salary. Here's what actually happens:

1. Subtract the standard deduction: $60,000 − $16,100 = **$43,900 taxable income**
2. First $12,400 taxed at 10% = **$1,240**
3. Next $31,500 ($12,401–$43,900) taxed at 12% = **$3,780**
4. **Total federal income tax: $5,020**
5. **Effective (actual) federal tax rate: 8.4%** — not 22%

The 22% bracket is your *marginal* rate — the rate on your next dollar of income. Your *effective* rate is almost always lower.

## FICA Taxes: Social Security & Medicare Explained {#fica-taxes-social-security-and-medicare}

FICA stands for Federal Insurance Contributions Act. These are mandatory — there's no way to reduce or avoid them through deductions or filing status. Both you and your employer each pay half of the total FICA obligation.

### 2026 FICA Tax Rates (Employee Share)

| Tax | Rate | Wage Cap | Max Employee Tax |
|-----|------|---------|-----------------|
| Social Security | 6.2% | $184,500 | $11,439 |
| Medicare | 1.45% | No cap | Unlimited |
| Additional Medicare | 0.9% | Wages over $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ) | Varies |

**Total standard FICA rate: 7.65%** on wages up to $184,500.

### What the Social Security Wage Base Means For You

If you earn $184,500 or more in 2026, your Social Security withholding stops once you hit that threshold. Your last paychecks of the year will be noticeably larger because that 6.2% drops off. If you're in this income range, it's worth factoring this into your year-end budgeting.

The wage base increased from $176,100 in 2025 to $184,500 in 2026 — a jump of $8,400. That means higher-income workers pay roughly $520 more in Social Security tax this year compared to last.

### Additional Medicare Tax for High Earners

If you're single and earn over $200,000 (or married and earning over $250,000 combined), you pay an additional 0.9% Medicare surtax on wages above those thresholds. Your employer is required to start withholding this extra 0.9% once your wages from them cross $200,000 — but if you have multiple jobs or your combined household income crosses the $250,000 threshold, you may owe additional tax when you file.

### Self-Employed? You Pay Both Sides

W-2 employees pay 7.65% FICA. Self-employed workers pay the full 15.3% self-employment tax because they're functioning as both employer and employee. The IRS does allow self-employed individuals to deduct half of the self-employment tax from their taxable income, which softens the blow somewhat.

## How Pre-Tax Deductions Reduce Your Tax Bill {#how-pre-tax-deductions-reduce-your-tax-bill}

Pre-tax deductions are one of the most powerful — and underused — tools for maximizing take-home pay. Every dollar you put into a qualifying pre-tax account reduces your taxable income before federal and state income taxes are calculated. In most cases, it also reduces your FICA base (the exception is some employer-sponsored health plans, which are pre-tax for income tax but not FICA).

### 401(k) Traditional Contributions

Traditional 401(k) contributions reduce your federal and state taxable wages dollar-for-dollar.

| Annual Salary | 401(k) Contribution | Federal Tax Saved | State Tax Saved (avg) | Total Annual Savings |
|--------------|--------------------|-----------------|-----------------------|---------------------|
| $75,000 | 5% ($3,750) | $450–$825 | $150–$195 | $600–$1,020 |
| $75,000 | 10% ($7,500) | $900–$1,650 | $300–$390 | $1,200–$2,040 |
| $100,000 | 5% ($5,000) | $1,100–$1,200 | $200–$275 | $1,300–$1,475 |
| $100,000 | 15% ($15,000) | $3,300–$3,600 | $600–$825 | $3,900–$4,425 |

The 2026 401(k) contribution limit is **$24,500** for workers under 50 and **$31,000** for workers 50 and older (including the $6,500 catch-up provision). If you're not maxing this out and you can afford to, the tax savings alone make it worth a serious look.

**Important:** Roth 401(k) contributions do NOT reduce your current taxable income. The benefit of Roth is tax-free growth and withdrawals in retirement. Neither is universally better — it depends on whether you expect to be in a higher or lower tax bracket in retirement.

### Health Insurance Premiums

Most employer-sponsored health insurance plans are set up as Section 125 cafeteria plans, meaning your premium contributions are pre-tax. A typical employee contribution of $300/month ($3,600/year) can save $500–$800 in combined federal income tax and FICA taxes annually, depending on your tax bracket.

### HSA (Health Savings Account) — The Triple Tax Advantage

An HSA paired with a High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) is one of the most tax-efficient savings vehicles in existence. HSA contributions offer a "triple tax advantage":

1. Contributions are **pre-tax** (reduce taxable income)
2. Growth is **tax-free**
3. Withdrawals for qualified medical expenses are **tax-free**

The 2026 HSA contribution limits are **$4,400 for self-only coverage** and **$8,750 for family coverage**.

### FSA (Flexible Spending Account)

FSAs work similarly to HSAs for tax purposes, but they're "use it or lose it" with a carryover limit of **$680 in 2026**. The 2026 FSA contribution limit is **$3,400**. FSAs are available through employers even without an HDHP, making them accessible to more workers.

### Pre-Tax Deduction Savings Summary

| Deduction | 2026 Limit | Income Tax Saved (22% bracket) | FICA Saved | Total Saved |
|-----------|-----------|-------------------------------|-----------|------------|
| 401(k) max (under 50) | $24,500 | $5,390 | $0* | $5,390+ |
| HSA (self-only) | $4,400 | $968 | $337 | $1,305 |
| HSA (family) | $8,750 | $1,925 | $669 | $2,594 |
| FSA | $3,400 | $748 | $260 | $1,008 |
| Health insurance ($300/mo) | $3,600/yr | $792 | $275 | $1,067 |

*401(k) contributions reduce income tax but not FICA. Health insurance and HSA contributions reduce both.

## Pay Frequency Comparison: Weekly vs Biweekly vs Monthly {#pay-frequency-comparison}

The frequency you're paid doesn't change your annual income — but it does change your cash flow, how you're taxed at the federal level (since withholding is computed per period), and how you budget.

### Pay Frequency Table: $80,000 Annual Salary, Single Filer, California

| Pay Frequency | Periods/Year | Gross Per Period | Est. Net Per Period |
|---------------|-------------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Weekly | 52 | $1,538 | ~$1,077 |
| Biweekly | 26 | $3,077 | ~$2,154 |
| Semi-monthly | 24 | $3,333 | ~$2,333 |
| Monthly | 12 | $6,667 | ~$4,667 |

### Why Biweekly Pay Creates a "Bonus" Month

If you're paid biweekly, you receive 26 paychecks per year. Most months have two paychecks — but twice a year, you'll receive three paychecks in a single month. Many smart budgeters treat that third check as a windfall: an extra mortgage payment, emergency fund top-up, or debt payoff boost. On a $75,000 salary, that third paycheck is worth roughly $2,100 after taxes.

### The Honest Picture: What California Has That Texas Doesn't

The tax comparison is real and significant. But the full picture includes:

- **Property taxes:** Texas property tax rates average 1.6%–2.4% of assessed value annually. California's Prop 13 caps increases, keeping many long-term homeowners' rates very low. On a $500,000 home, Texas property tax can easily run $8,000–$12,000/year versus $5,000–$7,000 in California.
- **Housing prices:** In many California cities (especially the Bay Area), income taxes are partly offset by higher salaries in the tech and finance industries.
- **Overall cost of living:** Texas cities, particularly Austin, have seen rapid price appreciation. The gap between TX and CA cost of living is narrowing in some metros.

For most workers on standard salaries in both states, Texas wins on take-home pay. The margin grows significantly as income increases.

## How to Maximize Your Take-Home Pay in 2026 {#maximize-take-home-pay}

These are the practical, legal moves that actually make a difference — not vague advice, but specific actions and the dollar amounts they're worth.

### 1. Contribute the Maximum to Your 401(k)

The 2026 contribution limit is $24,500 (under 50) or $31,000 (50 and over). Even if you can't max it out, every dollar you contribute reduces your taxable income dollar-for-dollar. A 5% contribution on a $75,000 salary ($3,750/year) saves roughly $600–$1,000 in combined federal and state taxes annually.

### 2. Open and Max an HSA (If Eligible)

If your employer offers a High Deductible Health Plan, an HSA is a no-brainer. The 2026 limits are $4,400 (self-only) and $8,750 (family). The triple tax advantage makes this one of the best financial tools available to working Americans.

### 3. Check Your W-4 and Update It

The W-4 form determines how much federal tax your employer withholds. Many people haven't updated theirs since starting their job — and if you've gotten married, had children, bought a home, or started a side business since then, your withholding is almost certainly wrong. The IRS has a free Tax Withholding Estimator at irs.gov that can tell you exactly what to put on your W-4 to avoid a surprise bill or a big refund you could have been using throughout the year.

### 4. Consider Relocating to a Lower-Tax State

If remote work is an option, moving from a high-tax state to a no-income-tax state can be worth $5,000–$15,000 per year at higher incomes. This isn't a minor consideration — it's a genuine life-altering financial decision that's worth running the numbers on.

### 5. Use All Available Pre-Tax Benefits

Beyond the 401(k) and HSA, check whether your employer offers:
- **Dependent Care FSA** — up to $5,000 pre-tax for child or elder care
- **Commuter Benefits** — up to $340/month (2026 limit) for transit/parking, pre-tax
- **Group Life Insurance** — premium for up to $50,000 coverage is typically pre-tax
- **Section 529 Payroll Deduction** — some employers allow this; deductible in some states

### 6. Understand the Bonus Withholding Rate — And Plan Around It

Bonuses and supplemental wages are withheld at a flat **22% federal rate** (or 37% if the bonus exceeds $1 million in the same year). This doesn't mean you'll necessarily owe 22% — your actual tax rate may be lower, and you may get some back when you file. But knowing this helps avoid the "why was my $5,000 bonus only $3,700?" shock.

### 7. Consider Timing Your Income (If Self-Employed or Commission-Based)

If you're self-employed or earn a significant portion of your income through commissions or bonuses, the timing of when you receive income can affect which tax year — and therefore which brackets — it falls into. This is a conversation worth having with a CPA if your variable income exceeds $20,000 annually.

### 8. If You Have Multiple Jobs, Adjust Your W-4s Accordingly

The W-4 instructions assume each job is your only job. If you work multiple jobs, the combined income may push you into a higher bracket — but each employer is withholding separately based on that job's income alone. Result: you often end up under-withheld. The IRS W-4 has a "Multiple Jobs" section specifically for this situation.

## Frequently Asked Questions {#frequently-asked-questions}

### How much tax is taken out of my paycheck?

For most workers in 2026, total withholdings (federal, state, and FICA) fall between 18% and 35% of gross pay depending on income, state, and filing status. At $50,000 filing single in a moderate-tax state, expect 20–24% total withholding. At $100,000, expect 27–32%. At $150,000 in California, total withholding can reach 38–42% of gross pay.

### What is the Social Security wage base for 2026?

The Social Security wage base for 2026 is **$184,500**. You pay the 6.2% Social Security tax on every dollar up to this amount. Once your wages exceed $184,500 in a calendar year, Social Security withholding stops for the rest of the year. Medicare's 1.45% continues on all wages with no cap.

### What is the 2026 standard deduction?

The 2026 federal standard deduction is **$16,100 for single filers**, **$32,200 for married filing jointly**, and **$24,150 for head of household**. Seniors aged 65 and over can claim an additional **$2,050** (single) or **$1,650 per qualifying spouse** (married). Additionally, the new Senior Bonus Deduction allows an extra deduction of up to $6,000 for eligible taxpayers 65 and older, phasing out above $75,000 (single) or $150,000 (married).

### How does a 401(k) contribution affect my paycheck?

Every dollar you put into a traditional (pre-tax) 401(k) reduces your federal and state taxable income by that dollar. On a $75,000 salary in the 22% federal bracket, contributing 10% ($7,500) saves approximately $1,650 in federal income tax plus whatever your state tax savings are. Your paycheck decreases, but by less than the contribution amount because you're keeping money that would have gone to taxes.

### Which states have no income tax in 2026?

Nine states collect no broad-based wage income tax: Alaska, Florida, Nevada, South Dakota, Tennessee, Texas, Wyoming, and Washington (wage income). New Hampshire also has no wage income tax. Workers in these states take home $2,000–$10,000+ more per year compared to high-tax states like California or New York, depending on salary level.

### What are the 2026 federal income tax brackets for single filers?

For single filers in 2026: 10% on income up to $12,400; 12% on $12,401–$50,400; 22% on $50,401–$105,700; 24% on $105,701–$201,775; 32% on $201,776–$256,225; 35% on $256,226–$640,600; 37% above $640,600. Remember these are marginal brackets — only the income in each bracket pays that rate. Most middle-income workers have an effective rate of 12–22%.

### What is the difference between gross pay and net pay?

Gross pay is your total earnings before any deductions. Net pay (take-home pay) is what remains after federal income tax, state income tax, Social Security (6.2%), Medicare (1.45%), and any pre-tax or post-tax deductions are subtracted. For most middle-income workers, net pay is 68–80% of gross pay. In high-tax states at higher incomes, it can drop below 65%.

### How does health insurance affect my paycheck?

Employer-sponsored health insurance premiums paid through a Section 125 cafeteria plan are pre-tax, reducing both your income tax and FICA taxable wages. For example, $300/month ($3,600/year) in health insurance premiums reduces your annual federal income tax by $792 (at 22% bracket) and your FICA by $275 — a combined savings of roughly $1,067 annually.

### How is my paycheck calculated for a bonus or supplemental income?

Bonuses are withheld at a flat 22% federal rate (or 37% for amounts over $1 million). State supplemental rates vary — California uses 10.23%, New York uses 11.7%, Texas has no state supplemental rate. Your actual tax liability on the bonus is determined when you file your annual return. You may owe more or receive a partial refund depending on your total income for the year.

### What does it mean if my paycheck went down even though my salary didn't change?

Several things can cause this: (1) Your wages crossed the Social Security wage base — but wait, this would make your check *larger*, not smaller. (2) Your wages crossed the $200,000 threshold and the 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax kicked in. (3) Your health insurance or 401(k) contribution amounts changed at open enrollment. (4) A new city or county tax went into effect in your jurisdiction. Check your pay stub line-by-line and compare it against the previous period to identify exactly what changed.

### Is a paycheck calculator accurate?

A well-built paycheck calculator using official 2026 IRS tables, the $184,500 Social Security wage base, and current state withholding schedules will typically come within 1–3% of your actual withholding. The main sources of variation are local taxes (not all calculators include these), supplemental income, W-4 elections beyond basic filing status, and employer-specific payroll timing. For tax planning purposes, these estimates are more than adequate.

### How do biweekly paychecks work for tax withholding purposes?

When you're paid biweekly, your employer calculates withholding on each $X paycheck as if that's a recurring biweekly salary. The IRS withholding tables are designed to result in approximately the right total withholding for the year when applied this way. You get 26 paychecks a year — meaning two months each year you receive three paychecks instead of two.

## Paycheck & Tax Glossary {#paycheck-and-tax-glossary}

**Adjusted Gross Income (AGI)** — Your gross income minus specific deductions like student loan interest, IRA contributions, and self-employment taxes. AGI is the starting point for calculating most tax deductions and credits.

**Biweekly Pay** — A pay schedule of 26 paychecks per year, issued every two weeks. The most common pay frequency for US workers.

**Effective Tax Rate** — Your total tax bill divided by your total income. Always lower than your marginal rate because lower-bracket income is taxed at lower rates.

**FICA** — Federal Insurance Contributions Act. The law requiring employers and employees each to contribute to Social Security (6.2%) and Medicare (1.45%).

**Filing Status** — Single, Married Filing Jointly, Married Filing Separately, or Head of Household. Determines your standard deduction and tax bracket thresholds.

**FSA (Flexible Spending Account)** — An employer-sponsored pre-tax account for medical or dependent care expenses. "Use it or lose it" with a carryover limit of $680 in 2026.

**Gross Pay** — Total earnings before any deductions.

**HSA (Health Savings Account)** — A triple-tax-advantaged savings account for medical expenses, available to those enrolled in a High Deductible Health Plan. Unlike FSAs, HSA balances roll over indefinitely.

**Head of Household** — A filing status for unmarried individuals who pay more than half the cost of maintaining a home for a qualifying person. Offers wider tax brackets than Single status.

**Marginal Tax Rate** — The tax rate applied to your last (highest) dollar of income. Your "tax bracket." This rate only applies to income above that bracket's threshold, not your entire income.

**Medicare Tax** — A 1.45% payroll tax on all wages, with an additional 0.9% on wages above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (married). Funds federal health insurance for Americans 65 and older.

**Net Pay** — Take-home pay. Gross pay minus all taxes and deductions.

**Pre-Tax Deduction** — A deduction subtracted from gross pay before taxes are calculated. Reduces both income tax and, in most cases, FICA. Examples: traditional 401(k), HSA, health insurance, FSA, commuter benefits.

**Progressive Tax System** — The US federal income tax structure in which higher income is taxed at progressively higher rates, applied only to the income within each bracket — not the entire income.

**Roth 401(k)** — Contributions made after taxes, providing no current tax reduction but allowing tax-free withdrawals in retirement. Not a pre-tax deduction.

**SDI (State Disability Insurance)** — A mandatory California payroll tax (approximately 1.1% in 2026) that funds short-term disability and Paid Family Leave benefits.

**Semi-Monthly Pay** — 24 paychecks per year, issued twice per month (typically the 1st and 15th, or 15th and last day of the month).

**Social Security Tax** — A 6.2% payroll tax on wages up to the 2026 wage base of $184,500. Funds retirement and disability benefits. Employers match this 6.2%.

**Standard Deduction** — A flat dollar amount that reduces your taxable income automatically, without needing to itemize individual deductions. In 2026: $16,100 (single), $32,200 (married filing jointly), $24,150 (head of household).

**Supplemental Wage Withholding** — A flat 22% federal withholding rate applied to bonuses, commissions, and other non-regular income. Your actual tax on this income is determined when you file.

**W-4 (Employee's Withholding Certificate)** — The IRS form filed with your employer to determine how much federal income tax to withhold from each paycheck. Should be updated whenever your personal or financial situation changes significantly.

**Wage Base** — The maximum amount of wages subject to a specific tax in a given year. The 2026 Social Security wage base is $184,500. There is no wage base for Medicare.
