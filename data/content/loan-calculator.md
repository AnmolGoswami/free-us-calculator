## 1. Amortized Loans (EMI) – The Standard for Mortgages

The **Equated Monthly Installment (EMI)** is the most common repayment method for personal and home loans. It uses the reducing balance method to ensure that as you pay off your principal, the interest you owe decreases.

### The EMI Mathematical Formula
To calculate a precise monthly payment, our engine uses the following industry-standard formula:

`EMI = [P × r × (1 + r)^n] / [(1 + r)^n - 1]`

**Where:**
- **P** = Principal Loan Amount (The total sum borrowed)
- **r** = Monthly Interest Rate (Annual Rate / 12 / 100)
- **n** = Loan Tenure in Months (Years × 12)

## 2. Deferred Loans & Compound Growth

A **Deferred Loan** (or Bullet Loan) is unique because the borrower does not make regular payments. Instead, the principal and the accumulated interest are paid in a single lump sum at the end of the term.

### Compounding Frequency Impact
In 2026, "Interest on Interest" is the most powerful force in finance. The more frequently your loan compounds, the more you will owe at maturity.

`A = P (1 + r/n)^(nt)`

**Key Compounding Options:**
- **Daily Compounding:** Often used in high-yield savings.
- **Monthly Compounding:** The standard for most credit-based deferred products.
- **Yearly Compounding:** Common in long-term government bonds.

## 3. Bond Analysis & Time Value of Money (TVM)

The **Time Value of Money** is the concept that $1,000 today is worth more than $1,000 a year from now due to its potential earning capacity. This is critical for Bond investors calculating **Present Value (PV)**.

### Zero-Coupon Bond Pricing Formula
If you know the future payout of a bond, you use this formula to decide what price you should pay for it today:

`PV = F / (1 + r)^n`

**Insights for Investors:**
- If the **Market Yield** goes up, the **Bond Price** goes down.

## Expert Financial Strategy for 2026

- **The 30% Rule:** Never allow your total monthly debt (EMI) to exceed 30% of your gross monthly income.
- **The Buffer Principle:** When calculating a loan, always add a 0.5% "Interest Buffer" to account for potential variable rate hikes.
